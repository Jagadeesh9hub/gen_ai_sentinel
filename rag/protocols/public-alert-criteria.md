# Public Alert Issuance Criteria

Criteria and approval flow for issuing public safety alerts.

## When to Issue
Issue a public alert when there is a credible threat to life or health in a defined area, when multiple correlated reports indicate a single developing event, or when a Tier 2+ hazmat, active fire, or evacuation condition is confirmed or strongly suspected. Err toward early shelter-in-place advisories for airborne hazards.

## Human Approval Requirement
Every public-facing alert requires human approval before transmission. The system drafts the alert text and proposes it; the on-duty coordinator reviews, edits if needed, and approves. Alerts are never sent automatically.

## Content Standards
An alert must state the hazard, the affected area, the recommended protective action (for example, shelter in place and close windows), and that updates will follow. Keep language plain, calm, and free of speculation. Avoid naming individuals or unconfirmed causes.

## Confidence and Evidence
Each proposed alert carries a confidence score and the evidence behind it (anomaly signal, forecast, escalation probability, and the protocol citation). Low-confidence proposals are surfaced for review but flagged as advisory.
